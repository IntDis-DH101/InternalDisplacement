Middle East & North Africa


Comparing Existing and New Internally Displaced People

Geographically, Libya and Egypt, which are neighboring countries, simultaneously have the lowest total number of IDPs and low amounts of new internal displacement compared to all the other MENA countries, but as we looked closer into the amount of new internal displacement relative to their own existing, we notice that Egypt had an significant increase in displacement in 2020 while Libya maintained relatively low increases in internal displacement. Given this finding, we decided to investigate more into Egypt and Libya to determine potential political conflicts, natural disasters, and governmental influence that have contributed to the rise and stability in internal displacement. On the other hand, in our other three MENA countries of interest, Syria, Iraq, and Yemen, which are all located in the Arabian Peninsula, we noticed that since these countries already have significantly high numbers of internally displaced people (IDPs), their new internal displacement may seem insignificant. However, as we explore their actual number of IDPs, the number of new IDPs from Syria is quite large – in the millions – while Iraq and Yemen have lower levels of additional internal displacement in 2020. Through these areas of interest, we will be investigating more deeply into each country’s underlying roots for the high levels of IDPs and the subsequent political and environmental drivers of the high and low amounts of new internal displacement recorded in 2020. We will be using aspects of the IDMC data set to gain greater insight into the specific attributes and recent circumstances of these MENA countries, such as their implementation of policies and distribution of governmental resources, to overall develop a stronger understanding of how each country has potentially tackled internal displacement and its cascading effects on both IDPs and the countries themselves.

Internal Displacement Index and Population


Nations with a Low Population are More Vulnerable to Internal Displacement

The visualization is organized in an ascending order of the country’s population and we can see that the bigger the population of the country, the higher the IDI value. This means that the population is a determining factor of the country’s IDPs, concluding that a higher population leads to better socioeconomic, environmental and political conditions. Countries with a higher IDI value are less vulnerable to internal displacement and include a functioning government to be responsible for policy making, environmental sustainability, socio economic development, security and political stability. In this visualization, we can see Egypt has the highest IDI value compared to Libya with the lowest IDI value. Since Libya has the least population in the MENA (Middle East and North Africa) region, the country is more susceptible to unsustainable environmental practices which may lead to natural disasters, impoverished regions, and severe crises. The selection of this visualization is a side-by-side circle comparison that focuses on the Environmental Sustainability Indicator (ESI) in the MENA region. The ESI is calculated based on the country’s environmental risks which include natural hazards and natural disasters. Based on the five MENA countries in our visualization, the average value amongst the countries is 0.49858 which indicates that the MENA region is in mediocre condition because of the existing climatic and demographic conditions. According to research conducted by Panorama, this region is considered to be a “host to extensive semi-arid and desert areas” which poses severe future risks as it can lead to water crises and chronic shortages (Borghesi & Ticci 2019). Additionally, the listed countries in our findings are “low- and- middle-income” which has a direct and indirect correlation to global warming, causing a weak adaptability to environmental risks (Borghesi & Ticci 2019). We can also see that Syria had the largest ESI value of 0.6691 and Iraq had the lowest ESI value of 0.3950. As a result of the Syrian civil war and the global pandemic, there is a possibility of a slowdown in economic productivity and reduced carbon emissions causing a decrease in population and GDP. Iraq is one the world’s fifth vulnerable countries in terms of high risk to climate change as it is currently suffering from “soaring temperatures and acute water scarcity” as well as an unstable government structure (Younis 2022). There have been ongoing talks with Turkey and Iran to reach a consensus on water management as Iraq is known to poorly control water supply (Younis 2022). This makes it difficult for policy implementation to combat climate change and improve water management which is shifting increasing migration to urban areas of Iraq (Younis 2022).

Socioeconomic Development


Quality of Life and Population Growth

The selection of this visualization is a side-by-side circle comparison that focuses on the Socioeconomic Development Indicator (SDI) in the MENA region. The SDI is calculated based on the country’s economic development measures which include GDP, population, and social development. Based on the five MENA countries in our visualization, the average SDI value is 0.6284 which shows that the MENA region is comparatively better than the average ESI value within the region. This means that the MENA region includes countries with a relatively higher GDP, population growth and quality of life in terms of health and education. However the consequences of climate change have a negative impact on the economic activities of the country. We can also see that Libya has the largest SDI value of 0.7240 and Yemen has the lowest SDI value of 0.4700. Yemen, classified as a war-torn country, has suffered from a sunken economy and “lack of basic services” has put education, health, water and electricity on hold (Mottaghi 2016). This increases high poverty rates and limited livelihood opportunities and according to “World Bank Document”, more than 10 percent are Internally Displaced Persons (Mottaghi 2016).

The Security and PSI is calculated based on the country’s political stability, governance and rule of law. Based on the five MENA countries in our visualization, the average Security and PSI value is 0.03334 which shows that the MENA region exhibits a weak government structure making it difficult for policy implementation on climate change. As a result, these countries have no security and have to defend themselves against the “consequences of climate change” in order to avoid unsustainable life conditions which increase IDPs (Borghesi & Ticci 2019). We can also see that Egypt has the largest Security and PSI value of 0.1286 and Yemen has the lowest PSI value of 0. Although the chart shows Egypt to have a higher PSI value, it is comparatively small the size of the population of the country (102,334,000). Due to the rapid population growth and climate change, Egypt is facing increasing poverty, unemployment, Nile water shortage, threats to food security, and weak policy implementation (Dunne 2020). Additionally, the land in Egypt is becoming less arable and receiving less rainfall due to climate change, which plays a major role in food insecurity (Dunne 2020). As a result of the war, Yemen is the least urbanized country in MENA and has widened gaps as a whole causing IDPS to travel far distances to access markets and health & education services (Semnani 2020).







GDP Compared to Safety


Why Iraq is an Outlier to the High GDP with High WGI Trend

To get insight into potential relations to the quality of life of IDPs we investigated the size of the economy via GDP in comparison to the political stability of the country. As seen on FIGURE X, four of the five MENA countries followed a pattern of high GDP with high WGI values (or low GDP with low WGI values). However, Iraq stuck out with a high GDP but a low WGI value; having a score of 1.9 which is in a similar range of countries that have GDP values that are only a fraction of Iraq’s.

Iraq is a country rich in crude oil production and is the second largest seat in oil exports right behind Saudi Arabia (EIA, 2022). In 2020 Crude oil revenue contributed to approximately 32% of the country’s GDP. (The World Bank, 2021) Despite Iraq earning a significant amount of revenue from oil, they lack diversification of investments and pour money into its inflated public sector and political influence. This in turn results in a stagnated economy, lacking improvements in the lives of the residing citizens. Due to the corrupt and unwise spending from the government, displaced individuals and those who need support are unable to gain access to necessities, resulting in protests and riots. (Lüdtke, 2022) With constant political conflicts and ineffective government investments, Iraq’s WGI value fails to improve.
