Internal Displacement

A Humanistic Approach to Internal Displacement

An Introduction

“How was it that a man could not walk into his own property, visit the grave of his wife, eat the fruit forty generations of his ancestors’ toil, without moral consequence?” (Abulhawa 33). This quote delves deep into our project on internal displacement, and poses as an example of the livelihood of those forced to leave their homes. Internally displaced people do not benefit from the protection that refugees do; in fact, most of them experience a turbulent transition to a new life. Many of those who are displaced cannot return home due to factors such as conflict and violence, socioeconomic and environmental sustainability. There are currently 84 million displaced people in the world according to UNHCR from which 55 million are internally displaced people, more than half of the population displaced. 48 million as of 2020 (Global Report on Internal Displacement, 2021) were living in internal displacement due to conflict and violence. After analyzing our data set Internal Displacement Values 2020, from the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, we deduced that the greater the population and GDP of the country, the more likely the state is to have a function government, this includes infrastructure, policy implementation, addressing internal displacement and strong socioeconomic conditions. This also shows that the lower the security and political stability indicator, the more likely a country is to experience internal displacement and be susceptible to natural disasters. .
